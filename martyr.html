Martyrdom

Bhagat Singh was just 23 years old when he was executed by the British on 23rd March 1931. He was hanged along with his comrades, Shivaram Rajguru and Sukhdev Thapar, in the Lahore Central Jail. The execution was a result of their revolutionary activities and the assassination of a British police officer named John Saunders.

Bhagat Singh's martyrdom had a profound impact on the Indian independence movement. His death was seen as a symbol of sacrifice and courage, and it galvanized the Indian people to continue their fight against colonial rule. The day of his execution, 23rd March, is celebrated in India as Martyrs' Day, to honor the sacrifice of Bhagat Singh and his comrades.

Bhagat Singh's last days in jail were marked by his unwavering commitment to the cause of Indian independence. He spent his time in jail reading books on socialism and revolutionary movements, and he wrote extensively on his ideas and beliefs. He also went on a hunger strike to protest against the inhumane treatment of political prisoners, which lasted for 116 days and brought international attention to the Indian independence movement.

Bhagat Singh's martyrdom also had a significant impact on the political landscape of India. His ideas on socialism and economic justice continue to inspire activists and revolutionaries around the world. His legacy has been kept alive by numerous books, films, and plays, which celebrate his life and contributions to the Indian independence movement.
